\par The paper titled "Performance analysis and comparison of PoW, PoS and DAG-based blockchains" by Bin Cao, Zhenghui Zhang, Daquan Feng, Shengli Zhang, Lei Zhang, Mugen Peng, and Yun Li, presents a comparative study of the performance of PoW, PoS, and DAG-based blockchains using several metrics, including transaction throughput, confirmation time, block size, energy consumption, and security. The paper provides a detailed analysis of each algorithm's strengths and weaknesses and concludes that PoS and DAG-based blockchains offer higher transaction throughput and lower energy consumption than PoW-based blockchains. However, they also note that PoW-based blockchains offer higher security guarantees than the other two algorithms
\par \emph{The paper "Comparative Review of the Blockchain Consensus Algorithm Between Proof of Stake (POS) and Delegated Proof of Stake (DPOS)"} by Sheikh Munir Skh Saad and Raja Zahilah Raja Mohd Radzi discusses two consensus algorithms used in blockchain technology: Proof of Stake (PoS) and Delegated Proof of Stake (DPoS). The authors compare and contrast the two algorithms, highlighting their key features, advantages, and limitations.

The authors then discuss DPoS, which is a modified version of PoS that involves delegating the validation process to a select group of validators, who are elected by stakeholders. They argue that DPoS is more secure than PoS, as it reduces the risk of centralization and the "nothing-at-stake" problem, and provides faster transaction confirmation times..

ABSTRACT
This paper provides a comparative analysis of three consen-
sus algorithms used in blockchain technology: Proof of Work
(PoW), Proof of Stake (PoS), and Delegated Proof of Stake
(DPoS). The paper first defines and explains each consensus
algorithm and its workings, followed by a comparison of their
strengths and weaknesses. The comparison includes factors
such as security, decentralization, scalability, energy efficiency,
and community involvement. The paper also discusses real-
world examples of blockchains that use each consensus algo-
rithm and the impact it has on their performance. Finally, the
paper concludes with a discussion on the future of consen-
sus algorithms and their potential evolution in the context of
blockchain technology.

CHAPTER 1
INTRODUCTION
Blockchain technology is a distributed, decentralized system that allows
multiple parties to interact with each other without the need for a central
authority. One of the critical components that make this possible is the
consensus algorithm. Consensus is the process by which all nodes in the
blockchain network agree on the validity of a transaction and confirm it in
the ledger.
In a decentralized platform like a blockchain, the consensus algorithm
ensures that all nodes on the network are in sync and agree on the current
state of the ledger. Without consensus, there would be no way to guarantee
the validity of transactions, and the entire system would be vulnerable to
attacks and manipulation.
Therefore, the importance of a consensus algorithm in blockchain cannot
be overstated. It is the foundation on which the entire system is built, and it
plays a critical role in ensuring the security, reliability, and trustworthiness
of the blockchain network.
1.1 Consensus Mechanism
Consensus mechanism is the process by which all nodes on a blockchain
network agree on the current state of the ledger. This is achieved through a
series of steps that are performed by the nodes to validate new transactions
and add them to the blockchain. The following are the typical steps involved
in a consensus mechanism:
2
Proof of Stake
• Transaction Propagation: Transactions are first broadcasted by a node
to the network. All nodes receive the transaction and validate it before
forwarding it to other nodes
• Verification: Nodes that receive the transaction verify its validity by
checking the digital signature, ensuring that the sender has sufficient
funds, and that the transaction is not a double-spend.
• Block Formation: Once a sufficient number of transactions have been
validated, they are grouped together in a block. A new block is created
by a node, which includes a cryptographic hash of the previous block,
creating a chain of blocks.
• Mining (for PoW): In the case of PoW, miners compete to solve a
cryptographic puzzle. The first miner to solve the puzzle is awarded
the right to create a new block and add it to the blockchain
• Validation (for PoS and DPoS): In the case of PoS and DPoS, nodes
are selected based on their stake in the network or by voting. These
nodes are responsible for validating the new block and adding it to the
blockchain
• Consensus: Once a new block is added to the blockchain, all nodes
on the network must agree on its validity. Nodes compare the hash of
the new block with the previous block and reach a consensus on the
current state of the ledger
• Confirmation: Once a block is confirmed, it cannot be altered or
deleted. The transaction is considered final and permanently recorded
on the blockchain

MACHINE LEARNING BASED IDS
With the rapid development of artificial intelligence in recent years, Ma-
chine Learning technology has played a vital role in combating threats in
cyberspace. The method of introducing machine learning into the network
intrusion detection system is mainly to convert the intrusion detection prob-
lem into the problem of pattern recognition and classification. In general,
ML is divided into three sub-domains: supervised, unsupervised, and rein-
forcement learning. Supervised learning requires labelled data for training
(both inputs and desired outputs). It discovers the relationship between data
and its class, while unsupervised learning is used when labelled data is not
available. These method find the hidden pattern in the data. Reinforcement
learning is based on a feedback mechanism. Here, computer program inter-
acts with the environment and learns by experience, figure 3.1[1]. Several
ML methods have been proposed for accurate intrusion detection. The most
commonly used methods are summarized in the following sub-sections.
